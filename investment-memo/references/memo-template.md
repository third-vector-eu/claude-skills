# Investment Memo Template

The complete annotated template for the 5-8 page memo. Use this as the structure for Phase 2 (Draft). Each section includes: what it is, what it should contain, and notes on common failure modes.

---

## Cover block (top of page 1)

A compact 4-line header, not a full cover page:

- **Company name** (working title if early).
- **Founder name(s)**.
- **Date** (use full date, e.g. 20 April 2026).
- **One-line positioning statement.** The "we help X do Y so that Z" or "We're building the X for Y" framing. No marketing adjectives.

---

## 1. Executive summary (½ page, written last)

A 5-7 sentence synthesis of the whole memo. Someone should be able to read only this section and know:

- What the business is.
- Who it serves.
- Why it will win.
- What stage it's at.
- What the ask is.

Do not restate the one-liner. Go one layer deeper. Favour specific over general.

**Common failure:** writing the exec summary before the memo is done. It has to come last.

---

## 2. Problem & target customer (¾ page)

The sharp end of the memo. Describe:

- **Who** — a specific persona or ICP, not "everyone who does X". Name the role, company stage, industry slice.
- **What hurts** — the specific pain. A story beats a statistic here. "A CX lead at a 200-person SaaS spends Monday morning hand-building dashboards in Zendesk that take two hours and are stale by Tuesday."
- **How they solve it today** — the status quo. Usually a combination of tools, workarounds, and quiet suffering. Be honest about it: if the status quo is "nothing, they live with it", say so — that's a red flag worth surfacing.
- **How often and how much** — frequency and cost of the pain. Ideally expressed in dollars, hours, or deals lost.

Tag each claim as **[Conviction]** or **[Hypothesis]**.

**Common failures:** describing a problem too broad to wedge into; describing a problem that is painful but not frequent enough to justify a purchase; describing a problem the founder has, not one the customer has articulated.

---

## 3. Why now (½ page)

The most under-written section in most memos. Answer: what has changed in the last 18-36 months that makes this possible or urgent now?

Valid "why nows" include:

- A technology capability that didn't exist before (e.g., "agent reliability crossed a usable threshold in 2024").
- A regulatory shift (new rules create demand for compliance tooling).
- A behavioural shift in the buyer (post-COVID remote work, AI budget creation).
- An incumbent stumbling (a category leader losing relevance, creating a migration moment).
- A cost curve crossing a threshold (inference costs, sensor costs, etc).

Invalid "why nows" include:

- "Customer support is broken." (True forever. Not a why-now.)
- "AI is exciting." (Too general.)
- "We have conviction." (That's not a why-now, that's a you-thing.)

**Test:** if the why-now paragraph could have been written verbatim three years ago, it isn't one.

---

## 4. Solution / wedge (¾ page)

**Clarity on the first narrow product.** Not the ten-year vision. The thing you'll ship in the next one or two quarters.

Include:

- **One-sentence description** of the wedge. If you need two sentences, it's not sharp enough.
- **How it works** — the actual mechanism, in plain language. What does the user do, what does the product do, what comes out the other side.
- **Why this wedge and not another** — the strategic choice. Why is this the best starting point?
- **The larger vision** — a paragraph at the end that sketches where the wedge leads over 3-5 years, so the reader can see the expansion path. Keep this brief. The wedge is the star.

**Common failure:** the wedge secretly being the whole vision. If it takes two years to ship, it's not a wedge.

---

## 5. Founder-market fit (½ page)

Why you, specifically. This section is about unfair advantage, not credentials.

Address:

- **Why you're a credible operator in this market.** What have you seen, built, or lived that gives you insight the average founder wouldn't have?
- **What's your "unfair"?** Network, scar tissue, privileged access to customers, a technical edge, a brand, distribution. Something others can't easily replicate.
- **What you're missing.** Honest. What will you need to hire, learn, or partner for?

Write this in first person and with humility. This section either feels earned or it feels like a résumé paragraph — only the first works.

**Common failure:** mistaking domain familiarity for domain advantage. "I used the product" is not founder-market fit.

---

## 6. Business model & pricing hypothesis (½ page)

How money flows, at the level of concrete numbers.

- **Who pays** — the actual budget holder, not the user.
- **What they pay for** — the unit of value. Seat? Usage? Outcome? Deflected ticket? Installed endpoint?
- **How much** — the ACV range you're targeting, and the logic behind it.
- **How often** — subscription, one-time, usage-metered, hybrid.
- **Contract shape** — monthly, annual, multi-year. Why.
- **Gross margin expectation** — back-of-envelope is fine, but show the math.

Tag the whole section as **[Hypothesis]** unless pricing has been tested with real buyers.

**Common failure:** pricing that doesn't match the GTM motion (e.g., $500 ACV with a 3-month enterprise sales cycle).

---

## 7. Go-to-market motion (¾ page)

How do the first 10, 50, and 200 customers get acquired? Specifics, not channels.

Cover:

- **First 10** — usually hand-crafted. Name the exact motion: who are the first 10 and how do you reach them? If the answer is "my network", name the 10 people.
- **First 50** — the repeatable motion starts here. What's the channel? What's the conversion mechanism? What's the messaging?
- **First 200** — the motion that either scales or gets replaced. Honest about which.
- **Sales motion** — self-serve, inside sales, or field sales. This should match the ACV.
- **What you've already validated.** Any customer conversations, signed LOIs, pilots, waitlist signups, early revenue. State them concretely.

**Common failure:** "we'll do content marketing and inbound" as the plan. That's not a plan, that's a hope.

---

## 8. Market & competition (½ page)

- **Market size.** Bottoms-up estimate beats top-down. Count the number of target accounts × ACV. Cite your assumptions.
- **Direct competitors.** Who solves this exact problem? Name them. What do they do well? What do they miss?
- **Indirect competitors.** What's the status quo the customer would use instead (internal tools, manual process, consultants, a different category of software)?
- **Your contrarian angle.** What do you believe about this market that most people in it don't? This is where differentiation lives.

**Common failure:** dismissing incumbents too quickly ("they're slow and bureaucratic"). Steelman them. If they're easy to beat, why haven't they been beaten yet?

---

## 9. Customer discovery status (¼ page)

Short, evidence-based section. Include:

- **How many customer conversations** have you had? (Distinguish: cold outreach calls, warm calls, paying or soon-paying customers.)
- **What did you actually hear** — including the things you didn't want to hear.
- **What signals are the strongest?** (E.g., "3 of 8 CX leaders unprompted asked when they could try it.")
- **Where you're thin** — the customer segments or questions you haven't probed.

This section is small but disproportionately weighty. Investors and advisors read it carefully.

**Common failure:** conflating "people thought it was cool" with product-market signal. Write it in a way that a skeptic would respect.

---

## 10. Risks, unknowns & falsification criteria (½ page)

The section most founders skip and most investors look for.

For each major bet in the memo, answer: **what would prove this wrong?**

Structure as a table or short list:

- **Bet:** what we believe.
- **Falsification criterion:** what we'd have to see to change our mind.
- **How we'll test it:** the lightest experiment that would produce signal.

Cover the 3-5 highest-load-bearing bets, not all of them. Typical ones:

- The customer will actually pay for this.
- The solution will work technically.
- The GTM motion will reach enough buyers cheaply enough.
- The market will grow, not shrink.
- We (the team) can execute this.

**Common failure:** listing generic risks ("execution risk", "market risk") without specifying what would prove the bet wrong. Generic risks are useless.

---

## 11. Milestones & first experiments (¼ page)

A short, concrete list of what you'll do in the next 30 / 60 / 90 days.

- **Next 30 days:** the highest-priority experiments from the falsification section.
- **Next 60 days:** product or GTM milestones that follow if the 30-day experiments succeed.
- **Next 90 days:** decision points — at this mark, what will you know, and what decision will you make?

Keep it to 3-5 items per horizon. A long list is a todo list, not a plan.

---

## 12. The ask (¼ page)

What do you actually need?

Options:

- **Capital** — specific amount and what you'd spend it on. Not a use-of-funds pie chart, a specific 18-month plan.
- **Hires** — the 1-2 roles that would most change the trajectory.
- **Advisors or introductions** — named, specific asks.
- **Design partners** — the ideal first 3-5 customers.
- **Nothing, bootstrapping** — in which case, explicitly say so and explain why bootstrapping is the right choice for this particular business.

This section is short but important. It tells the reader how to help.

**Common failure:** a vague "we're raising $X" without a specific plan for what it funds, or a bootstrapping note that sounds like resignation instead of conviction.

---

## Formatting rules (summary)

- 5-8 pages total. Tight.
- Full prose, no bullet-heavy slides-as-docs.
- Body: 11pt, line spacing 1.15.
- H1: bold 14pt, `0F172A`. H2: bold 12pt.
- Accent blue `2563EB` sparingly (section callouts, the Conviction/Hypothesis tags).
- One cover block at top. No imagery.
- Tag every major claim as **[Conviction]** or **[Hypothesis]**.
- File name: `{company_name}_investment_memo_{YYYY-MM-DD}.docx`.

## Style conventions

- **No em dashes (`—`).** Use commas, colons, semicolons, or periods instead. Em dashes create visual noise and are a reliable tell for machine-generated prose. Hyphens (`-`) and en dashes (`–`) are fine for number ranges (e.g. `€15-25k`, `2026-2027`).
- **Smart quotes on** (`"` `"` `'` `'`), not straight quotes.
- **Active voice** over passive. First-person plural for conviction claims ("we believe"), hypothesis framing for things not yet validated.
- **No empty adjectives.** Cut "revolutionary", "best-in-class", "world-class", "cutting-edge". Every adjective should either describe reality or declare a bet.
- **Cite sources by name + year** when you reference studies, stats, or market claims (e.g. "MIT NANDA 2025") so the reader can verify.
